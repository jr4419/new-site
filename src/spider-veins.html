Spider veins are dilated tiny blood vessels within the skin layer itself — less than 1 mm in diameter. They appear as red, blue, or purple branching patterns, starbursts, or linear threads on the surface of the skin.

Spider veins on the legs can be a marker of underlying hidden saphenous reflux. Up to 30–40% of patients with spider veins on the thighs or knees have an incompetent saphenous trunk feeding them. Treating only the spider veins in that situation means they will either recur quickly or worsen over time, because the underlying pressure isn't corrected.

Symptoms

Most patients with spider veins have no symptoms at all. Some, however, experience burning, itching, throbbing, leg fatigue, or bleeding.

Diagnosis

Evaluation is minimal but important. It begins with a visual inspection, and the most important question is whether there is underlying venous reflux — assessed with duplex ultrasound.

Treatments

Sclerotherapy — the gold standard

A sclerosant solution is injected directly into the vein through an ultra-fine 30-gauge needle. The solution irritates the vein's lining, causing it to spasm, collapse, and gradually be absorbed by the body over 4–8 weeks. Typically 2–4 sessions, spaced 4–8 weeks apart, are needed for optimal clearance.

Post-treatment:

  • Compression stockings (15–20 mmHg) for 3–7 days for leg spider veins (no compression needed for facial spider veins)
  • Walk 20–30 minutes immediately to help prevent deep vein thrombosis
  • Avoid heavy lifting, hot baths, saunas, flying, and sun exposure for 48 hours
  • Avoid aspirin, ibuprofen, and alcohol for 24–48 hours (they increase bruising)

Possible side effects include bruising, matting, hyperpigmentation, pain or burning, and phlebitis.

Laser therapy

Best for veins too small for sclerotherapy (under 0.3 mm), needle-phobic patients, facial spider veins, or residual veins after sclerotherapy.
