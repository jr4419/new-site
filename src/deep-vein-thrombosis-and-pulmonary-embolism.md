**Age is the single strongest risk factor:** incidence rises exponentially with age, and an 80-year-old is 40–50× more likely to have VTE than a 30-year-old. Women have slightly higher rates during reproductive years (pregnancy, oral contraceptives).

## Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)

A clot forms in the deep veins of the leg, most commonly in the venous valve pockets where flow is slowest.

### Clinical presentation

- One-sided leg swelling
- Calf pain
- Warmth
- Redness
- Bluish discoloration (in severe cases)

### Diagnosis

Compression ultrasound is the gold standard. The single most reliable finding is failure to fully compress the vein with gentle probe pressure.

## Pulmonary embolism (PE)

A clot from the leg breaks off, travels through the right heart, and lodges in the pulmonary arteries.

### Classic symptoms

- Sudden shortness of breath (most PEs present with isolated shortness of breath)
- Sharp chest pain that worsens with breathing
- Cough
- Coughing up blood
- Fainting

### Diagnosis

CT pulmonary angiography, with sensitivity and specificity above 95%. The finding is a filling defect (clot) within the pulmonary artery.

## Treatment of DVT and PE

- **Acute anticoagulation** is the backbone of treatment. DOACs are now preferred over warfarin — fewer interactions, no INR monitoring, and lower risk of major bleeding.
- **Thrombolysis** for high-risk PE only.
- **Catheter-directed therapies** for intermediate-risk PE with strain on the heart's right side.
- **Inferior vena cava (IVC) filter** in selected cases.

## Post-thrombotic syndrome

Up to 40–50% of DVT patients develop chronic venous insufficiency from the damage the clot causes to the vein valves. Prevention of VTE relies on pharmacologic and mechanical prophylaxis for the highest-risk patients.
