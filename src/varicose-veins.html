Varicose veins are enlarged, twisted, bulging superficial veins (3 mm or larger) that have lost their ability to return blood efficiently to the heart. They are the visible sign of chronic venous insufficiency (CVI) — the underlying failure of the one-way valves in the leg veins.

They affect the superficial venous system, and are common: roughly 25–30% of women and 15–20% of men.

Risk factors

  • Female sex: hormonal influences (estrogen, progesterone) relax vein walls; pregnancy increases blood volume.
  • Pregnancy: hormonal and mechanical (the enlarged uterus compresses pelvic veins).
  • Age: progressive valve degeneration.
  • Family history: a strong genetic component — more than 70% have an affected first-degree relative.
  • Prolonged standing or sitting: gravity plus lack of calf-muscle pumping.
  • Obesity: increased intra-abdominal pressure impairs venous return.
  • Prior deep vein thrombosis (DVT): direct valve damage (post-thrombotic syndrome).

What patients actually feel

Early

  • Visible bulging veins — often the first thing noticed
  • Mild heaviness or tiredness in the legs at the end of the day
  • Sometimes completely asymptomatic (a purely cosmetic concern)

Moderate

  • Heaviness, aching, throbbing — classic "venous claudication"
  • Worse with prolonged standing, better with walking or elevation
  • Swelling around the ankles that improves overnight
  • Restless legs and nighttime leg cramps
  • Itching over the vein (venous eczema)

Advanced

  • Brownish skin discoloration around the ankle
  • Stasis dermatitis — dry, scaly, itchy, red skin
  • Lipodermatosclerosis — hardened, woody skin and fat (the "inverted champagne bottle" leg)
  • Atrophie blanche — white, scarred patches with tiny red dots
  • Venous ulcer — typically over the inner ankle; shallow, painful, and slow to heal

Diagnostic workup

The office assessment begins with a physical exam. Duplex ultrasound is the gold standard, performed while standing to maximize vein distension. It assesses reflux times (more than 0.5 seconds is abnormal; more than 1.0 second is severe), vein diameter, the deep veins (to rule out DVT), perforator location and size, and maps the course of reflux for treatment planning.

Treatment: a stepwise approach

Step 1 — Conservative therapy

Compression stockings are the foundation — they reduce symptoms and prevent progression, but must be properly fitted and worn consistently (on before getting out of bed, off at night). Other measures include leg elevation (15–30 minutes, 2–3× per day above heart level), daily walking (30 minutes) to activate the calf-muscle pump, avoiding prolonged standing or sitting beyond two hours, weight optimization, and not crossing the legs at the knees.

Step 2 — Endovenous ablation (for saphenous reflux)

  • Endovenous laser ablation (EVLA): 94–98% occlusion at 5 years.
  • Radiofrequency ablation (RFA).
  • VenaSeal (medical adhesive): seals the vein immediately, with no tumescent anesthesia or compression stockings required afterward — good for patients who can't tolerate compression or have needle phobia. Occlusion rates are ~92–96% at 2 years.

Step 3 — Treating the varices themselves

After the source of reflux is ablated, bulging tributaries often shrink partially on their own (30–50%), but residual symptomatic veins are common and can be treated with:

  • Phlebectomy (stab avulsion).
  • Foam sclerotherapy: a sclerosant is mixed into a foam and injected under ultrasound guidance; good for recurrent varicose veins and small-to-moderate tributaries, and may need 2–3 sessions.

Step 4 — Surgery

Reserved for complex cases.

Prevention — who should start early?

Those with a first-degree relative with varicose veins, prolonged-standing occupations (nurses, retail, teachers, surgeons), multiple pregnancies, or obesity. Preventive measures include compression stockings for high-risk individuals, a regular walking program, leg-elevation breaks during the day, and early evaluation at the first sign of bulging veins or new symptoms.
