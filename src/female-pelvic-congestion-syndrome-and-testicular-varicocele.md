## Clinical picture

### Pelvic congestion syndrome (PCS)

PCS is primarily characterized by chronic pain, a direct result of blood pooling in dilated pelvic veins. It is most often a dull ache, usually felt on one side, and is chronic — lasting at least 6 months. Unlike menstrual cramps, the pain does not follow a cyclical pattern, though it can worsen during menstruation. Because symptoms often overlap with other conditions, it is important to discuss them thoroughly with a healthcare professional for an accurate diagnosis.

### Varicocele

Many men with a varicocele have no symptoms at all. When symptoms do appear, they can include:

- **A dull ache or pain:** often described as heaviness or a "dragging" feeling in the scrotum, typically worse when standing or later in the day, and often improving when lying down.
- **A visible or palpable mass:** sometimes described as feeling like a "bag of worms" above the testicle.
- **Differently sized testicles:** the testicle on the affected side may be smaller.
- **Fertility problems:** a varicocele is linked to infertility in some men.

## Diagnosis

- Duplex ultrasound is often the first-line, non-invasive test.
- Venography (an X-ray of the veins) is used as the definitive diagnostic tool in more complex cases.

## Treatment

Both conditions can be effectively treated with a minimally invasive procedure called gonadal vein embolization. Using a catheter, coils or a sclerosant agent are placed into the faulty vein to block it off. This redirects blood flow to healthy veins, relieving pain and pressure. It is a common treatment for both, with studies showing high technical success and significant symptom relief in women with PCS and men with varicoceles.
