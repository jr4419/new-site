Chronic venous insufficiency (CVI) is a common condition in which the veins in your legs have difficulty returning blood to your heart. It happens when the one-way valves inside the veins become damaged, allowing blood to flow backward and pool in the lower legs. Over time this can lead to a range of symptoms and skin changes.

What causes CVI?

The primary cause is venous hypertension — abnormally high pressure in the leg veins. This is often the result of faulty valves (incompetence) or a previous blood clot, known as deep vein thrombosis (DVT).

Risk factors

  • History of DVT: as many as 30% of people who have a DVT may develop CVI within 10 years.
  • Age and sex: more common in people over 50 and in women.
  • Lifestyle: prolonged sitting or standing, lack of exercise, smoking, and obesity.
  • Other factors: pregnancy, family history, and being tall.

Recognizing the symptoms

Common symptoms: a dull ache, heaviness, or cramping in the legs; swelling in the lower legs and ankles, especially after standing; itching and tingling; and visible varicose veins. Pain typically worsens when standing and improves when you elevate your legs.

Skin changes over time may include:

  • Discoloration — a reddish-brown color from blood pooling
  • Thick and hard skin — a condition called lipodermatosclerosis
  • Inflamed or flaky skin — known as stasis dermatitis
  • Ulceration — slow-healing open sores (venous stasis ulcers), usually near the ankles — the most severe complication

Diagnosis

A vascular surgeon can often diagnose CVI with a physical exam and a review of your symptoms, examining your legs while you stand. To confirm the diagnosis and rule out problems like a blood clot, a duplex ultrasound may be recommended — a non-invasive test that checks the structure of your veins and the direction of blood flow.

Treatment options

1. Self-care and lifestyle changes

  • Compression stockings: a cornerstone of treatment; proper fitting is essential.
  • Exercise and movement: regular walking strengthens the calf-muscle pump; avoid long periods of sitting or standing.
  • Leg elevation: raise your legs above heart level when resting.
  • Weight management and skin care: help prevent skin breakdown and infection.

2. Non-surgical procedures

  • Sclerotherapy: a solution or foam is injected into the damaged vein, causing it to seal and eventually disappear.
  • Endovenous thermal ablation: laser or radiofrequency energy heats and closes the affected vein from the inside.

3. Surgery

Needed for fewer than 10% of patients with CVI. Options include removing or tying off the problematic veins (ligation and stripping) or, in rare severe cases, bypassing them.

Prognosis

CVI is progressive and tends to worsen over time, but it can be successfully managed when treatment begins early. Following recommended self-care and seeking treatment when needed can significantly relieve discomfort and prevent the condition from worsening.

When to see a doctor

Contact a vascular surgeon if you experience sudden increases in leg pain or swelling, fever, redness, or open sores on your leg.
