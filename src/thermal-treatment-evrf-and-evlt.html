Both endovenous radiofrequency ablation (EVRF) and endovenous laser treatment (EVLT) are minimally invasive, highly effective "gold standard" treatments for varicose veins. They work on the same principle — using heat to close off the diseased vein — but achieve it with different energy sources (laser light vs. radiofrequency waves), which leads to some technical differences.

Both are performed under local anesthesia to close faulty veins, and their procedural steps are very similar.

Step by step

1. Pre-procedure evaluation and preparation

The doctor performs a detailed duplex ultrasound to map your veins, confirm reflux (backward blood flow), and plan the treatment. You change into a gown and lie on the procedure table, positioned for the leg and vein being treated.

2. Ultrasound-guided access

Using ultrasound, the doctor finds the best entry point, usually near the knee or calf. The skin is numbed with a local anesthetic, a tiny puncture is made, and a thin catheter is guided into the diseased vein.

3. Tumescent anesthesia

A large volume of cool saline and local anesthetic is injected around the entire length of the vein. This numbs the vein to prevent pain during treatment and creates a protective "cushion" of fluid that insulates the surrounding skin and tissue from the heat.

4. Energy delivery and vein ablation

A laser fiber or radiofrequency catheter is passed through the catheter. As it is slowly pulled back, the energy heats the vein, causing the collagen in the wall to shrink and seal it closed. This part typically takes about 5–20 minutes.

5. Post-procedure care

The catheter is removed and the small puncture site is covered with a bandage (stitches are usually not needed). A compression stocking is applied and worn continuously for a set period, often 48 hours.

6. Immediate recovery and walking

You will be encouraged to walk for 5–30 minutes shortly afterward to promote blood flow and reduce the risk of complications. You will need someone to drive you home. Most people return to normal activities within a day or two, but should avoid heavy lifting and strenuous exercise for about two weeks.

7. Follow-up

A follow-up ultrasound is typically scheduled within two weeks to confirm the treated vein is successfully closed.

Patient experience and recovery

Recovery is generally quick for both procedures, with most people returning to normal activities within 1–2 days.

  • Post-procedure discomfort: EVRF is often associated with less pain, bruising, and hematoma than EVLT because of its lower, more controlled heat — though bruising is still common with both.
  • Procedural experience: both are outpatient procedures using ultrasound guidance, with a thin catheter inserted through a tiny incision that usually doesn't require stitches.

How to choose

Both are excellent, safe options, and the best choice depends on the specific anatomy of your veins (size, location, and complexity).

  • EVRF may be preferred for larger veins to minimize post-procedure bruising and pain.
  • EVLT may be recommended for veins with complex shapes, those that are deeper, or those below the knee.
