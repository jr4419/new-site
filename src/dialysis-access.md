A well-functioning access delivers blood flow rates of 300–500 mL/min during dialysis. Common complications include stenosis (narrowing), thrombosis (clotting), infection, and aneurysm formation. Stenosis is the most common culprit: progressive narrowing of the vessel is the root cause of 85% of clotted accesses.

## Symptoms

- Arm swelling
- Pain
- Prolonged bleeding after dialysis
- Difficulty cannulating the access

## Diagnosis

Close monitoring by a vascular professional is critical to catch problems early.

## Management

- **Angioplasty (PTA):** a balloon catheter is advanced over a wire to the narrowed segment and inflated to open the lesion.
- **Stenting:** reserved for stenosis that returns immediately after the balloon is deflated, or for recurrent stenosis.

## Why surveillance and prevention matter

Stenosis develops silently over weeks to months. By the time it causes thrombosis, the access is often lost or requires an urgent procedure. Surveillance catches stenosis early — while it is still treatable with simple angioplasty — preventing the far more serious event of thrombosis.

A patient whose access is monitored and surveilled properly will have fewer hospitalizations, fewer missed treatments, fewer catheters, and an access that lasts months to years longer than one that relies on "waiting for problems."
