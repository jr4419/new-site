Peripheral arterial occlusion is blockage or severe narrowing of the arteries that supply the limbs, usually from atherosclerosis.

PAD is the leading cause of lower-limb amputations and is considered more lethal than many cancers. About half of PAD patients are asymptomatic until late stages, so many cases go undiagnosed — the true number may be over 20 million in the US. Roughly 21.7% of women and 14.8% of men aged 65 and older have PAD, and PAD is associated with a 3–6× increased risk of heart attack, stroke, and cardiovascular death.

Typical symptoms

  • Leg pain, cramping, or fatigue when walking
  • Relief with rest
  • Cold or pale limb
  • Weak pulses
  • In severe cases: rest pain, non-healing ulcers, and gangrene

Diagnosis

  • Ankle-brachial index (ABI) — a screening test for people with high cholesterol, high blood sugar, and high blood pressure
  • Doppler ultrasound
  • CT angiography or MR angiography if needed

Treatment

  • Smoking cessation, exercise therapy, antiplatelet therapy, and statins
  • Control of diabetes, hypertension, and lipids
  • Angioplasty, stenting, or bypass surgery if severe or limb-threatening

Prevention is key

  • Non-invasive ABI screening and arterial ultrasound evaluation in a vascular surgeon's office
  • 80% of amputations are preceded by a preventable foot ulcer
  • Smoking cessation, exercise, and statins can dramatically reduce progression
